Building Your
Data Foundation

10 min readGovernance and Growth

AI tools are only as good as the data they run on. You already know this intuitively. but most small businesses reach the scaling stage and discover their data is fragmented, inconsistent, and largely inaccessible to the tools they want to deploy. The pilot worked despite the data situation. Scaling will not.

Building a data foundation does not mean hiring a data engineer or purchasing a data warehouse. For most SMBs, it means three things: knowing what data you have, getting it into formats AI tools can actually use, and establishing simple practices that keep it clean going forward.

The Data Audit

Before you can improve your data situation, you need to know what you are working with. A data audit does not have to be sophisticated. It is a structured inventory of where your business data actually lives right now.

Data Audit Framework
Where Does It Live?List every system that holds business data: your CRM, accounting software, POS system, spreadsheets, email, project management tools, and any industry-specific platforms. Most businesses are surprised by how many systems they find.
Is It Structured?Structured data lives in rows and columns and can be exported as a CSV or connected via API. Unstructured data lives in documents, emails, and images. AI works best with structured data. Unstructured data requires extra processing steps.
How Old Is It?Data older than 18 months may no longer reflect your current business reality. For forecasting and pattern recognition, recency matters. Identify your freshest, most complete datasets first.
Who Owns It?Every dataset needs a named owner who is responsible for its accuracy. If nobody owns it, nobody maintains it. This is the single most common reason business data becomes unreliable over time.

The three problems that derail more AI scaling initiatives than any technical issue are siloed data, dirty data, and undocumented data. Understanding each one will help you prioritize where to invest your cleanup effort first.

The Three Data Problems
Siloed DataYour sales data is in your CRM. Your financial data is in QuickBooks. Your operational data is in spreadsheets. None of them talk to each other. AI tools that need a complete picture of the business cannot get one. The fix is not always integration. sometimes it is a weekly export routine that consolidates the data you need into one place.
Dirty DataDuplicate records, inconsistent naming conventions, missing fields, and outdated entries are the norm in most SMB systems. A CRM where the same customer appears three times with three different spellings cannot feed a reliable AI recommendation. Before scaling any AI initiative, run a data quality pass on the specific dataset it will use.
Undocumented DataData that exists but that only one person knows how to interpret is fragile. If your controller is the only person who understands how your accounting codes work, that data is effectively unavailable to any AI tool. Documentation does not have to be elaborate. a single-page data dictionary for each major dataset is enough.
The Fix SequenceAddress these in order: first siloed data (consolidation), then dirty data (quality), then undocumented data (documentation). Trying to clean dirty data before consolidating it means cleaning the same data twice. Get it in one place first.

Building Data Habits That Scale

The best data foundation is not the result of a one-time cleanup. It is the result of habits that keep data clean as it is created. These habits do not require new technology. They require new expectations about how your team enters and maintains data.

The Five Data Habits

Enter data once and enter it correctly. Establish naming conventions and enforce them. Assign ownership to every dataset. Set a monthly review cadence for data quality. Never let a spreadsheet become the system of record for mission-critical data. These five habits, consistently applied, will put you ahead of 90% of businesses your size within 12 months.

Vendor Evaluation
at Scale

10 min readGovernance and Growth

Your first pilot probably used an off-the-shelf tool you found through a recommendation or a Google search. That is the right approach for Phase 1. But as you scale, the vendor decisions get larger, more expensive, and harder to reverse. A bad vendor relationship at scale can cost you 12 months of progress and significant money.

Vendor evaluation is not about finding the best tool in a category. It is about finding the right tool for your specific situation, with a vendor who will still be there when you need support six months from now.

The Vendor Evaluation Framework

Eight Questions to Ask Every AI Vendor
1. Where Does My Data Go?Your data is your most valuable asset. Understand exactly where it is stored, who has access to it, whether it is used to train the vendor's models, and how it is deleted when you end the relationship. Get this in writing.
2. What Is the True Cost?The listed price is rarely the total cost. Factor in implementation time, training time, integration costs, and any per-seat or usage-based fees that kick in as you scale. Ask for a 12-month cost projection based on your expected usage.
3. What Does the Contract Lock You Into?Annual contracts with auto-renewal clauses are standard. Understand the cancellation terms, data portability rights (can you export your data if you leave?), and any exclusivity provisions before you sign.
4. What Is the Integration Story?Does this tool connect to the systems you already use? Native integrations are faster and cheaper than custom API work. Ask specifically about your existing tech stack and whether those integrations are maintained or community-built.
5. Who Is Your Support Contact?For SMBs, support quality often matters more than feature depth. A powerful tool with no support is dangerous. Ask who your named support contact is, what their response time SLA is, and whether support costs extra.
6. What Is the Vendor's Financial Health?AI startup failures are common. Before committing to a vendor for a mission-critical process, do a basic check: how long have they been operating, who are their investors, and do they have enterprise clients whose contracts would incentivize them to stay in business?
7. Can You Run a Paid Pilot?Any vendor worth working with will let you run a structured trial before committing to an annual contract. If a vendor refuses a pilot or trial period, that is a signal about how they treat customers once locked in.
8. What Does Offboarding Look Like?The easiest time to negotiate exit terms is before you sign. Ask specifically how to export your data, what the process is to cancel, and whether there are any fees associated with leaving. Vendors who make offboarding easy are vendors who are confident you will stay.

Build vs. Buy vs. Configure

As AI becomes more accessible, the build-vs-buy decision has a third option that most SMBs overlook: configure. Many AI platforms now allow significant customization without any code. Understanding where you are on this spectrum determines how much technical capability you need to bring in-house.

ApproachWhen to UseRiskTimeline
Buy (off-the-shelf)Standard process, well-served categoryLowDays to weeks
Configure (platform + customization)Unique workflow, existing platform fitsMediumWeeks to months
Build (custom development)No existing solution, proprietary advantageHighMonths to years
The Configuration Sweet Spot

For most SMBs scaling AI, the configuration path is underutilized. Platforms like Make, Zapier, and n8n allow you to connect AI capabilities to your existing tools without writing code. A configured workflow built on proven infrastructure carries a fraction of the risk of a custom build and can often be deployed in days rather than months.

Red Flags in Vendor Pitches

Walk Away When You Hear These
"The AI learns your business automatically"No AI learns your business without structured data, human feedback, and time. This claim signals a vendor more focused on selling than on setting realistic expectations.
"Implementation takes just a few hours"Any tool that meaningfully changes how your team works takes longer than a few hours to implement correctly. Underestimated implementation time is the leading cause of failed AI deployments.
"Our ROI is guaranteed"ROI from AI tools depends almost entirely on how your team uses them. No vendor can guarantee results they do not control. This claim is a sales tactic, not a business reality.
"You do not need technical resources"Every AI deployment requires someone who owns the configuration, the data connection, and the ongoing maintenance. That person does not have to be a developer but they do have to exist.

Change Management
Across Teams

9 min readGovernance and Growth

Scaling AI across an organization is not primarily a technology problem. It is a people problem. The tools are the easy part. Getting five different teams with five different workflows, comfort levels, and concerns to adopt new ways of working is where most scaling initiatives stall.

The businesses that scale AI successfully treat change management as a core competency, not an afterthought. They invest in it before the rollout begins and maintain it throughout. The ones that skip it spend months trying to reverse resistance that could have been prevented with two weeks of preparation.

Why People Resist AI Adoption

Resistance to AI is rarely irrational. Your employees have real concerns that deserve direct answers. The mistake most leaders make is either dismissing those concerns or avoiding them entirely. Both approaches backfire. The only thing that works is addressing them head-on, specifically, and early.

The Four Legitimate Concerns and How to Address Each
Job SecurityThis is the primary concern and the one most leaders handle worst. Do not say "AI will not replace anyone" if that is not true. Instead, be specific: which roles are being augmented, which tasks are being automated, and what those employees will do with the reclaimed time. Specificity builds trust. Vagueness destroys it.
Competency AnxietyEmployees worry they will not be able to use the new tools and that this will be used to evaluate their performance. Address this by framing AI adoption as a team capability, not an individual test. Training should be delivered in groups, not assigned individually. Peer learning reduces anxiety faster than formal instruction.
Workflow DisruptionPeople have spent years developing efficient personal workflows. Changing those workflows has a real productivity cost in the short term. Acknowledge this explicitly. Tell your team that the first 30 days will be slower, not faster, and that this is expected and planned for. Surprises kill morale. Expectations protect it.
Loss of Expertise ValueExperienced employees worry that AI will make their hard-won expertise irrelevant. The reality is the opposite. AI without expert human judgment produces mediocre outputs. Make this clear and make it visible: showcase examples where employee expertise improved AI outputs in ways the tool alone could not achieve.

On Change Champions

The change champion model works because peer credibility is more powerful than leadership authority when it comes to behavioral change. Identify one person per team who is respected by their colleagues, comfortable with technology, and willing to be publicly associated with the initiative. Give them early access, extra training, and public recognition. Their adoption signals to their teammates that this is safe and manageable.

Measuring Adoption, Not Just Outcomes

Most organizations measure AI success by outcomes: time saved, errors reduced, revenue generated. These are the right metrics long-term, but they are lagging indicators. In the first 90 days, measure adoption instead. Are people logging in? Are they using the features that drive value? Are they asking questions about how to do more with the tool? Adoption metrics tell you whether outcomes are coming. Outcome metrics tell you what already happened.

Warning

If adoption metrics are low at 45 days, do not wait until 90 days to intervene. Low adoption at 45 days almost always reflects an unaddressed concern or a training gap, not a tool problem. Go back to the people. Ask what is getting in the way. The answers are almost always actionable.

Building Internal
AI Capability

10 min readGovernance and Growth

The businesses that win the AI transition over the next five years are not the ones with the most tools. They are the ones that build the internal capability to evaluate, deploy, and iterate on AI faster than their competitors. That capability does not come from tools. It comes from people, processes, and institutional knowledge that compounds over time.

Internal AI capability is what makes your second initiative cheaper than your first, your third faster than your second, and your organization increasingly difficult to displace. It is the compounding advantage that separates AI leaders from AI followers.

The Four Pillars of Internal AI Capability

What Internal AI Capability Actually Looks Like
Pillar 1: Institutional KnowledgeEvery AI initiative produces lessons. What worked, what did not, what the data revealed, what the team learned. Organizations that systematically capture and share this knowledge get smarter with every initiative. Organizations that do not start from scratch every time. A simple internal wiki or shared document updated after every initiative is enough to start.
Pillar 2: AI Fluency Across RolesAI fluency does not mean everyone becomes a data scientist. It means every role in your organization understands how AI affects their work, can identify opportunities where AI could help, and knows how to evaluate whether an AI output is trustworthy. This fluency is built through exposure, not training courses.
Pillar 3: A Repeatable Evaluation ProcessThe first time you evaluate an AI tool, it takes weeks. The tenth time should take days. Building a repeatable vendor evaluation process, a standard pilot structure, and a consistent ROI measurement framework means each new initiative benefits from everything you learned before.
Pillar 4: A Named AI FunctionAt some point, AI initiatives need an owner who sits above any individual department. This does not have to be a dedicated hire. It can be a part-time responsibility assigned to a senior person. What matters is that someone has visibility across all AI initiatives, prevents duplication, and ensures organizational learning is captured and shared.

Building an AI Governance Framework

Governance sounds bureaucratic. In practice, it is simply the set of decisions you make in advance so that you do not have to make them in a crisis. A lightweight AI governance framework addresses four questions: what AI can be used for, who has authority to approve new AI tools, how AI outputs are reviewed before acting on them, and what happens when something goes wrong.

Lightweight AI Governance for SMBs
Approved Use CasesMaintain a short list of approved AI use cases and a short list of prohibited ones. Approved: drafting internal documents, generating reports, customer inquiry routing. Prohibited: making final hiring decisions, generating external legal documents without review, sharing proprietary data with unvetted AI tools. The list does not have to be long. It has to exist.
Approval AuthorityAny new AI tool that touches customer data, financial data, or employee data should require explicit approval from a named decision-maker before deployment. This prevents well-intentioned employees from inadvertently creating compliance or security risks. A simple one-page approval form is sufficient.
Output Review StandardsAI outputs should not go directly to customers or into mission-critical decisions without human review. Define the standard: who reviews, what they are checking for, and when review can be abbreviated as the tool proves reliable over time. Start with 100% review and relax it as trust is earned.
Incident ResponseDefine in advance what you do when an AI tool produces an incorrect output that affects a customer or a business decision. Who is notified, what is the remediation process, and how is the incident documented so it does not happen again? Having this defined before an incident means you respond from a plan, not from panic.
The Compounding Advantage

Organizations that build internal AI capability systematically find that their cost per AI initiative decreases by roughly 30% with each successive project. The institutional knowledge, the repeatable process, and the growing fluency of the team compound in the same way that any organizational competency compounds. The businesses that start building this capability now will have a structural advantage that is very difficult for later entrants to replicate.
