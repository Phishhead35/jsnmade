Picking the Right
First AI Project

9 min readFrom Pilot to Proof

The number one reason AI pilots fail is not technical. It is that they were working on the wrong problem from the start. A technically perfect AI solution to a low-value problem is still a failure. Choosing well is more important than executing well.

There are three criteria every good first AI project must meet. If your candidate project does not pass all three, move on. There are always better options.

The Three Criteria

The Pilot Selection Framework
1. High RepetitionThe task happens frequently. Daily, weekly, or at volume. AI ROI compounds with repetition. A task done once a quarter is a poor candidate. A task done 50 times a day is an excellent one.
2. Measurable OutcomeYou can define success in a number before you start. Time saved. Error rate reduced. Cost per unit lowered. If you cannot define the metric before you begin, you will not be able to prove it worked.
3. Low Failure RiskIf the AI gets it wrong, the consequences are manageable. Document drafting, internal reporting, and customer inquiry routing are low risk. Medical diagnosis and legal determinations are high risk. Start where a mistake is recoverable.
Bonus: Data Already ExistsThe best first projects have 6 to 12 months of historical data you can use immediately. You are not waiting to collect data, you are deploying against what you already have.

The Prioritization Matrix

When you have multiple candidates, score each one across two dimensions: how much value does solving it create, and how easy is it to implement with AI? Projects in the top-right quadrant are your first pilots.

Project TypeValueEaseVerdict
Document drafting & templatesHighEasy✓ Start here
Customer inquiry routingHighEasy✓ Start here
Demand forecastingVery HighMedium→ Phase 2
Custom AI model trainingVery HighHard✗ Not yet
Internal knowledge base Q&AMediumEasy→ Quick win
The Golden Rule

Pick the project where a 20% improvement is worth more than the cost of the pilot. That is your minimum bar. If you cannot estimate what 20% improvement in this area is worth annually, stop and do that math first.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

What Kills Pilots Before They Start
No Named OwnerEvery pilot needs one person accountable for results. Not a committee. Not the IT team. One person whose job it is to make this work.
Undefined Success"We will know it is working when it feels better" is not a success criterion. Define the number before day one.
Scope CreepThe pilot starts as "automate invoice processing" and becomes "rebuild our entire finance workflow." Stay on the original scope until you have proof of concept.
Building Custom When Off-the-Shelf ExistsFor a first pilot, there is almost always an existing tool you can deploy in days. Custom builds are for Phase 3, not Phase 1.

Running the 90-Day
Pilot

10 min readFrom Pilot to Proof

You have chosen your project. You have a named owner and a success metric. Now you need to run it in a way that produces clean, defensible data, because the output of a pilot is not just results, it is a business case for what comes next.

The 90-day structure is not arbitrary. Shorter pilots do not produce enough signal. Longer pilots lose momentum and accumulate scope creep. Ninety days is the window where real patterns emerge and organizational patience holds.

The Four Phases

90-Day Pilot Structure
Days 1 to 15: SetupInstall the tool. Train the owner. Establish your baseline measurement: what does the process look like today, before AI? Document this rigorously. Your baseline is the foundation of your ROI calculation.
Days 16 to 45: RunDeploy in a controlled environment. Not company-wide. A defined subset: one team, one region, one process flow. Collect data weekly. Resist the urge to declare success or failure early.
Days 46 to 75: MeasureCompare against baseline. Calculate actual vs. projected improvement. Identify the friction points: what is working, what is not, what would need to change to scale. Document everything, including the failures.
Days 76 to 90: DecidePrepare a clean go/no-go recommendation with data. If it worked: propose Phase 2 with specific scope and budget. If it did not: document why and propose the prerequisite work. Both outcomes are valuable.
On Measuring the Baseline

Most organizations skip baseline measurement because it is tedious. This is a fatal mistake. Without a clear before picture, you cannot prove the after. Spend days 1 to 5 doing nothing but documenting the current state of the process you are improving. Time it. Count it. Cost it out.

Managing People During the Pilot

Technical failure is rare. People failure is common. The employees whose workflow is changing have real concerns about their jobs, about being evaluated, about learning something new. Ignoring these concerns is the fastest way to kill a pilot that would otherwise have succeeded.

Before you launch the pilot, have a direct conversation with the team: "This tool handles the repetitive parts of your job so you can focus on the work that actually requires your judgment. We are not replacing anyone. We are upgrading how the team operates." Say it once, clearly, and mean it.

The Weekly Check-In Rhythm

A 15-minute weekly check-in with the pilot owner prevents small problems from becoming pilot-killers. Ask three questions every week: What is working? What is not? What do you need to keep going? Keep it lightweight enough that people do not dread it.

What to Do When It Is Not Working

If the pilot is not producing results by day 45, do not wait until day 90 to acknowledge it. Diagnose early: is the tool wrong, or is the process wrong? A messy process fed into AI produces messy outputs faster. Fix the process first, then rerun the pilot.

Measuring Results &
Making the Case to Scale

8 min readFrom Pilot to Proof

The pilot is done. You have data. Now you need to turn that data into a decision and present it in a way that gets the right answer from leadership. This lesson is about measurement, storytelling, and the specific structure of a go/no-go recommendation that actually moves people.

The Four Numbers That Matter

You do not need a 40-slide deck. You need four numbers, clearly stated, with the math behind each one.

The ROI Calculation Framework
1. Time SavedHours per week x average fully-loaded hourly cost x 52 weeks. Example: 10 hours/week x $45/hour x 52 = $23,400/year per employee affected.
2. Error ReductionCost per error x errors per month x reduction percentage x 12. Even a 30% reduction in rework cost justifies most pilots. Errors are almost always more expensive than people think.
3. Revenue ImpactHarder to measure but most compelling to leadership. Did faster response times increase conversion? Did better data quality improve forecasting? Quantify conservatively and state your assumptions.
4. Pilot CostTool cost + implementation time + training time. Be honest. Understating costs destroys credibility. Overstating them makes the ROI even more impressive when actuals come in lower.
The 10x Rule

A pilot needs to show at least 10x projected ROI to justify scaling investment. If your pilot cost $5,000 and it is producing $50,000 in annual value, that is your case. Anything below 5x is a marginal result that will struggle to get budget approval in most organizations.

Structuring the Go/No-Go Recommendation

The most effective recommendation follows a simple structure: one page, four sections. Leadership does not read long documents. Give them the summary and be ready for the questions.

SectionWhat to Include
What We TestedOne sentence. The tool, the process, the timeframe.
What We FoundThe four numbers. Baseline vs. result. Clean and simple.
The RecommendationGo or no-go. If go: proposed scope, cost, and timeline for Phase 2. If no-go: why, and what needs to change first.
The AskOne specific decision. A budget approval, a headcount allocation, a timeline commitment. Make it easy to say yes.

When to Scale vs. When to Pause

Scale vs. Pause Decision Framework
Scale When:Results exceeded baseline by 20% or more. ROI is 10x or better. The team using it wants to keep using it. The process it supports is stable. You have a named owner ready to lead Phase 2.
Pause When:Results are mixed or inconsistent. The underlying process is chaotic. Key personnel are resistant. The tool requires more customization than anticipated. Budget or leadership alignment is unclear.

Pausing is not failure. A well-documented pause that identifies what needs to change first is more valuable than a forced scale that produces mediocre results. The goal is not to prove AI works. It is to make AI work for your specific situation.
