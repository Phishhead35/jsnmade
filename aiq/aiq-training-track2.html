Picking the Right
First AI Project

⏱ 9 min read ◈ From Pilot to Proof

The number one reason AI pilots fail is not technical. It is that they were working on the wrong problem from the start. A technically perfect AI solution to a low-value problem is still a failure. Choosing well is more important than executing well.

There are three criteria every good first AI project must meet. If your candidate project doesn't pass all three, move on. There are always better options.

The Three Criteria

The Pilot Selection Framework
1. High Repetition The task happens frequently. Daily, weekly, or at volume. AI ROI compounds with repetition. A task done once a quarter is a poor candidate. A task done 50 times a day is an excellent one. Ask: how many times does this happen per week?
2. Measurable Outcome You can define success in a number before you start. Time saved. Error rate reduced. Cost per unit lowered. Revenue per rep increased. If you can't define the metric before you begin, you won't be able to prove it worked when you're done.
3. Low Failure Risk If the AI gets it wrong, the consequences are manageable. Document drafting, internal reporting, and customer inquiry routing are low risk. Medical diagnosis, financial advice, and legal determinations are high risk. Start where a mistake is recoverable.
Bonus: Data Already Exists The best first projects have 6–12 months of historical data you can use immediately. You're not waiting to collect data. you're deploying against what you already have. This is why data infrastructure matters so much in the assessment.

The Prioritization Matrix

When you have multiple candidates, score each one across two dimensions: how much value does solving it create, and how easy is it to implement with AI? Projects in the top-right quadrant. high value, easy to implement. are your first pilots.

Project Type Value Ease Verdict
Document drafting & templates High Easy ✓ Start here
Customer inquiry routing High Easy ✓ Start here
Demand forecasting Very High Medium → Phase 2
Custom AI model training Very High Hard ✗ Not yet
Internal knowledge base Q&A Medium Easy → Quick win
The Golden Rule

Pick the project where a 20% improvement is worth more than the cost of the pilot. That's your minimum bar. If you can't estimate what 20% improvement in this area is worth annually, stop and do that math first. It's the most important number in your business case.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

The most common first-pilot mistakes aren't about technology. they're about scope and ownership. Avoid these patterns and you're already ahead of most organizations that attempt AI pilots.

What Kills Pilots Before They Start
No Named Owner Every pilot needs one person who is accountable for results. Not a committee. Not "the IT team." One person whose job it is to make this work.
Undefined Success "We'll know it's working when it feels better" is not a success criterion. Define the number before day one. Everything else is noise.
Scope Creep The pilot starts as "automate invoice processing" and becomes "rebuild our entire finance workflow." Stay on the original scope until you have proof of concept.
Building Custom When Off-the-Shelf Exists For a first pilot, there is almost always an existing tool you can deploy in days. Custom builds are for Phase 3, not Phase 1.

Running the 90-Day
Pilot

⏱ 10 min read ◈ From Pilot to Proof

You've chosen your project. You have a named owner and a success metric. Now you need to run it in a way that produces clean, defensible data. because the output of a pilot isn't just results, it's a business case for what comes next.

The 90-day structure is not arbitrary. Shorter pilots don't produce enough signal. Longer pilots lose momentum and accumulate scope creep. Ninety days is the window where real patterns emerge and organizational patience holds.

The Four Phases

90-Day Pilot Structure
Days 1–15: Setup Install the tool. Train the owner. Establish your baseline measurement. what does the process look like today, before AI? Document this rigorously. Your baseline is the foundation of your ROI calculation. Do not skip this step.
Days 16–45: Run Deploy in a controlled environment. Not company-wide. A defined subset. One team, one region, one process flow. Collect data weekly. Resist the urge to declare success or failure early. Let the data accumulate.
Days 46–75: Measure Compare against baseline. Calculate actual vs. projected improvement. Identify the friction points. what is working, what is not, what would need to change to scale. Document everything, including the failures.
Days 76–90: Decide Prepare a clean go/no-go recommendation with data. If it worked: propose Phase 2 with specific scope and budget. If it didn't: document why and propose the prerequisite work. Both outcomes are valuable.
On Measuring the Baseline

Most organizations skip baseline measurement because it's tedious. This is a fatal mistake. Without a clear before picture, you can't prove the after. Spend days 1–5 doing nothing but documenting the current state of the process you're improving. Time it. Count it. Cost it out. That data is worth more than the AI tool itself.

Managing People During the Pilot

Technical failure is rare. People failure is common. The employees whose workflow is changing have real concerns. about their jobs, about being evaluated, about learning something new. Ignoring these concerns is the fastest way to kill a pilot that would otherwise have succeeded.

Before you launch the pilot, have a direct conversation with the team: "This tool is here to handle the repetitive parts of your job so you can focus on the work that actually requires your judgment. We're not replacing anyone. we're upgrading how the team operates." Say it once, clearly, and mean it.

The Weekly Check-In Rhythm

A 15-minute weekly check-in with the pilot owner prevents small problems from becoming pilot-killers. Ask three questions every week: What's working? What's not? What do you need to keep going? That's it. Keep it lightweight enough that people don't dread it.

What to Do When It's Not Working

If the pilot isn't producing results by day 45, don't wait until day 90 to acknowledge it. Diagnose early: is the tool wrong, or is the process wrong? Often the process needs to be cleaned up before AI can work on it. A messy process fed into AI produces messy outputs faster. Fix the process first, then rerun the pilot.

Measuring Results &
Making the Case to Scale

⏱ 8 min read ◈ From Pilot to Proof

The pilot is done. You have data. Now you need to turn that data into a decision. and present it in a way that gets the right answer from leadership. This lesson is about measurement, storytelling, and the specific structure of a go/no-go recommendation that actually moves people.

The Four Numbers That Matter

You don't need a 40-slide deck. You need four numbers, clearly stated, with the math behind each one.

The ROI Calculation Framework
1. Time Saved Hours per week × average fully-loaded hourly cost × 52 weeks. This is your annual productivity value. Example: 10 hours/week × $45/hour × 52 = $23,400/year per employee affected.
2. Error Reduction Cost per error × errors per month × reduction percentage × 12. Even a 30% reduction in rework cost justifies most pilots. Errors are almost always more expensive than people think.
3. Revenue Impact Harder to measure but most compelling to leadership. Did faster response times increase conversion? Did better data quality improve forecasting? Quantify conservatively and state your assumptions.
4. Pilot Cost Tool cost + implementation time + training time. Be honest. Understating costs destroys credibility. Overstating them makes the ROI even more impressive when actuals come in lower.
The 10× Rule

A pilot needs to show at least 10× projected ROI to justify scaling investment. If your pilot cost $5,000 (tool + time) and it's producing $50,000 in annual value. that's your case. Anything below 5× is a marginal result that will struggle to get budget approval in most organizations.

Structuring the Go/No-Go Recommendation

The most effective recommendation follows a simple structure. one page, four sections. Leadership doesn't read long documents. They read executive summaries and ask questions. Give them the summary and be ready for the questions.

Section What to Include
What We Tested One sentence. The tool, the process, the timeframe.
What We Found The four numbers. Baseline vs. result. Clean and simple.
The Recommendation Go or no-go. If go: proposed scope, cost, and timeline for Phase 2. If no-go: why, and what needs to change first.
The Ask One specific decision. A budget approval, a headcount allocation, a timeline commitment. Make it easy to say yes.

When to Scale vs. When to Pause

Scale vs. Pause Decision Framework
Scale When: Results exceeded baseline by 20%+ · ROI is 10× or better · The team using it wants to keep using it · The process it supports is stable · You have a named owner ready to lead Phase 2
Pause When: Results are mixed or inconsistent · The underlying process is chaotic · Key personnel are resistant · The tool requires more customization than anticipated · Budget or leadership alignment is unclear

Pausing is not failure. A well-documented pause that identifies what needs to change first is more valuable than a forced scale that produces mediocre results across the organization. The goal is not to prove AI works. it's to make AI work for your specific situation.
